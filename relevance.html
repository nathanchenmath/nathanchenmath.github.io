Nathan Chen is a Research Connections

Mathematical Context

What This Research Is About

My research focuses on fundamental questions in algebraic geometry: How can we measure the complexity of geometric objects? What are the constraints on how these objects can be transformed? How do different types of geometric structures relate to each other?

These questions might seem abstract, but they connect to deep problems across mathematics. The techniques I develop and the results I prove provide tools and insights that other mathematicians use in their own work, often in unexpected ways.

Below, I explain how my specific research areas connect to other branches of mathematics, and why these connections matter for advancing mathematical knowledge.

Connections to Other Mathematical Fields

Number Theory & Arithmetic Geometry

My work on abelian surfaces and their degrees of irrationality connects directly to arithmetic geometry. Abelian varieties are fundamental objects in number theory—they encode arithmetic information and appear in the study of elliptic curves, modular forms, and Diophantine equations.

When I study rational maps from products of curves to surfaces, I'm working on problems that arise naturally in arithmetic geometry. The techniques I develop for measuring irrationality provide new tools for understanding the arithmetic properties of these geometric objects.

While my work doesn't directly solve the Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture, it contributes to the broader understanding of abelian varieties and their rational maps—knowledge that other researchers use in their work on these deep number-theoretic problems.

Birational Geometry & Moduli Theory

My research on Fano varieties and their birational properties is central to the classification of algebraic varieties. Fano varieties are fundamental building blocks in birational geometry, and understanding their automorphism groups and rational maps is crucial for the broader classification program.

When I investigate whether Fano varieties have finite order birational automorphisms, I'm contributing to our understanding of the birational geometry of these important objects. This work connects to moduli theory—the study of how geometric objects can be classified and parameterized.

My results on measures of irrationality provide new invariants that help distinguish between different types of varieties, which is essential for understanding the structure of moduli spaces.

Complex Geometry & Hodge Theory

My work on holomorphic one-forms and fibrations over abelian varieties connects to complex geometry and Hodge theory. The existence and properties of holomorphic forms are fundamental to understanding the complex structure of algebraic varieties.

When I study fibrations over abelian varieties, I'm working on problems that arise naturally in the study of complex manifolds. The techniques I develop contribute to our understanding of how complex geometric structures can be organized and classified.

This work provides new insights into the relationship between algebraic and complex geometry, which is central to many problems in both fields.

Algebraic Geometry & Commutative Algebra

My research on complete intersections and their subvarieties connects directly to commutative algebra. Complete intersections are fundamental objects that arise naturally in algebraic geometry, and understanding their properties is crucial for many problems in the field.

When I study curves on complete intersections, I'm working on problems that require deep understanding of both geometric and algebraic structures. The techniques I develop often involve sophisticated commutative algebra methods.

My work contributes to the broader understanding of how algebraic and geometric structures interact, which is fundamental to algebraic geometry as a whole.

Why This Research Matters

For the Mathematical Community

My research contributes to fundamental questions in algebraic geometry that many other mathematicians work on. The techniques I develop—for measuring irrationality, studying birational automorphisms, and understanding complete intersections—provide tools that other researchers can use in their own work.

Each result I prove opens new questions and suggests new directions for investigation. My work on Fano varieties contributes to the broader classification program in birational geometry, while my studies of abelian surfaces provide new insights into arithmetic geometry.

The collaborative nature of my research—working with experts across different areas of algebraic geometry—demonstrates how modern mathematics advances through the synthesis of ideas and techniques from multiple perspectives.

Mathematical Impact

My work provides new invariants and techniques for studying algebraic varieties. The measures of irrationality I study give us new ways to distinguish between different types of geometric objects, which is essential for understanding their classification and properties.

By studying rational maps between varieties, I'm contributing to our understanding of how complex geometric objects can be transformed and simplified. This work connects to fundamental questions about the structure and classification of algebraic varieties.

My results often provide the foundation for other researchers to build upon, either by extending my techniques to new settings or by using my results to solve related problems in algebraic geometry and related fields.

Specific Research Contributions

Measures of Irrationality

My work develops new techniques for measuring the complexity of algebraic varieties:

  • New Invariants: Developing measures that help distinguish between different types of varieties
  • Bounds and Estimates: Proving results about how complex certain geometric objects can be
  • Connections to Arithmetic: Linking geometric complexity to arithmetic properties of varieties
  • Tools for Classification: Providing new methods for understanding the structure of algebraic varieties

Fano Varieties & Birational Geometry

My research reveals fundamental properties of important geometric objects:

  • Automorphism Groups: Understanding the symmetries and self-maps of Fano varieties
  • Rational Maps: Studying how varieties can be transformed and simplified
  • Complete Intersections: Investigating the structure of fundamental geometric building blocks
  • Moduli Spaces: Contributing to our understanding of how varieties can be classified

Mathematical Connections

My work connects different areas of mathematics:

  • Algebraic Geometry ↔ Arithmetic Geometry: Using geometric methods to study arithmetic problems
  • Birational Geometry ↔ Moduli Theory: Contributing to the classification of algebraic varieties
  • Complex Geometry ↔ Algebraic Geometry: Studying the relationship between complex and algebraic structures
  • Commutative Algebra ↔ Algebraic Geometry: Using algebraic techniques to solve geometric problems
